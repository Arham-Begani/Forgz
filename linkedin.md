# LinkedIn Post Writing Guide for AI Agents

This file tells you exactly how to write LinkedIn posts that get real engagement. Read every section before writing a single word.

---

## Who You're Writing For

Before writing anything, ask: who is this person's audience? A post for founders reads differently than one for engineers or HR managers. The voice, examples, and hooks all shift based on who's scrolling. Always tailor the content to that specific audience — if you try to speak to everyone, you'll connect with no one.

---

## The Golden Rule: Sound Like a Human, Not a Press Release

The number one reason posts flop is that they sound like they were written by a committee. Write the way you'd talk to a smart colleague over coffee. Not dumbed down. Not stiff. Just clear, direct, and real.

If you read it out loud and it sounds like a corporate script — rewrite it.

---

## Language Rules (Non-Negotiable)

### Words and phrases to NEVER use:
- Passionate / I'm passionate about
- Excited to share / Thrilled to announce
- Game-changer / Game-changing
- Synergy / Synergize
- Leverage (as a verb — "leverage our strengths")
- Innovative / Innovation (unless backed by a specific example)
- Strategic / Strategically
- Thought leader / Thought leadership
- Ecosystem (in a business context)
- Value-add / Value-driven
- Holistic approach
- Move the needle
- Circle back / Reach out (as buzzwords)
- Best-in-class
- Robust (to describe anything other than wine)
- Seamless experience
- At the end of the day
- Deep dive (as a standalone phrase)
- Unlock [your potential / success / growth]
- Empower / Empowering
- Impactful
- Take it to the next level
- Pivot (overused)
- Disruption / Disruptive

### What to do instead:
- Use plain words. Say "grew" not "scaled." Say "cut costs" not "optimized spend."
- Use real numbers. "We reduced onboarding time from 3 days to 4 hours" beats "we improved efficiency significantly."
- Be specific. "Our team of 6 shipped this in 2 weeks" beats "our talented team delivered outstanding results."
- Write in first person and active voice. "I built" not "was instrumental in building."

## Post Structure That Works

### 1. The Hook (Line 1 — most important)

LinkedIn cuts off posts after 2-3 lines. The first line must make someone stop scrolling. It should be one of these:

- A short, unexpected statement: *"I almost didn't post this."*
- A counterintuitive opinion: *"Hustle culture is making your team worse."*
- A specific number or result: *"We hit $1M ARR with zero paid ads."*
- A direct question that stings a little: *"Are you hiring for skills or just job titles?"*
- A short story opener: *"My manager called me into his office. I thought I was fired."*

Never start with: "I'm excited to share..." or "In today's fast-paced world..." or "As a [job title], I often find myself..."

### 2. The Body

Keep it short and scannable. Use line breaks generously — no walls of text.

Formats that work:
- Short story with a lesson at the end
- List of observations (not a bulleted list — write it as numbered points or single lines)
- Contrarian take followed by your reasoning
- A mistake you made + what you learned
- A simple "here's what I noticed" observation from real work

Every paragraph should be 1-3 lines max. Leave white space. People skim.

### 3. The Ending

End with one of these:
- A question that invites a real answer (not "what do you think?" — be specific: "Has this happened to you?")
- A short, punchy takeaway line
- A call to action only if it's natural and not pushy

Do NOT end with: "Follow me for more content like this" or "Like and share if you agree" or "Drop a 🔥 in the comments."

## Post Types That Get High Engagement

### The Personal Story Post
Share something real that happened to you. Wrap a professional insight in a human moment. Vulnerability works. The format is: what happened → what you thought → what you learned. Keep it honest — people can tell when it's manufactured.

### The Contrarian Take
Challenge a popular belief in your industry. State the common view first, then your counterpoint, then back it up with real experience or data. Engage actively with people who disagree — that's what keeps it going.

### The Practical List
Share something immediately useful — a cheat sheet, a checklist, a set of things you wish you'd known. Present it in a clean, simple format. No jargon. The test: can someone use this today?

### The Honest Observation
Something you noticed at work, in your industry, or in your own behavior. Short, direct, no framing. This is the "calling out a quiet truth" format. Often the simplest posts perform best.

### The Milestone or Thank You Post
Mark a real achievement or thank a specific group of people — without making it about yourself. Be specific about what the milestone means, not just that it happened. No self-congratulation; make it about the people or the work.

### The Hot Take / Poll
Ask a clear, specific question where people have real opinions. Works best when the answer isn't obvious. Engage with every comment.

## Formatting Rules

- Line breaks after every 1-3 sentences. White space is your friend.
- Emojis: use sparingly. One or two at most, only if they add something. Never use them as bullet points (the 👉 every line style is overplayed).
- Hashtags: 3-5 max, placed at the end. Use specific ones, not vague ones like #success or #motivation.
- Post length: 150-300 words is the sweet spot for most posts. Long-form (500-800 words) works for deep story posts. Under 100 words works for punchy observations.
- No bold text mid-post — it looks artificial and breaks the natural reading flow.
- Never use ALL CAPS for emphasis.

---

## Timing and Frequency

- Post 2-4 times per week. Consistency beats volume.
- Best times: weekday mornings (8–10 AM in the audience's timezone) and midday (12–1 PM). Midweek (Tuesday–Thursday) tends to outperform Monday and Friday.
- Reply to every comment within the first hour of posting — this signals engagement to the algorithm and keeps the post circulating.

---

## The Vibe Checklist (Run This Before Every Post)

Before finalizing, ask:

- [ ] Does line 1 make you want to read line 2?
- [ ] Is there a single buzzword or corporate phrase in here?
- [ ] Does it sound like something a real person would actually say?
- [ ] Is there at least one specific detail (number, name, situation)?
- [ ] Is the ending a natural stop, not a sales pitch?
- [ ] Would this person's specific audience find this useful or relatable?
- [ ] Is the post scannable, with no walls of text?

If all boxes are checked — post it.

---

## What Makes Posts Fail

- Opening with "I" (LinkedIn's algorithm deprioritizes this; also it's weak)
- Being vague when you could be specific
- Trying to sound smart instead of being clear
- Posts that are really just ads in disguise
- Ending with an aggressive CTA
- Using too many hashtags or emojis
- Writing about yourself without giving the reader anything

---

## One Last Thing

The posts people are unsure about often do best. The ones that feel too honest, too simple, or too short. Don't over-polish. Real always beats polished on LinkedIn.
